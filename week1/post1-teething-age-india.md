# What Age Do Babies Start Teething in India? Signs, Timeline & What to Do

It is 2 a.m. Your baby, who was sleeping through the night just last week, is screaming. Nothing is working — not the feeding, not the rocking, not the lullaby you have sung forty times. Your mother-in-law says it is gas. Your neighbour says it is teething. The parent group on WhatsApp has twelve different opinions.

If your baby is somewhere between three and seven months old, there is a real chance that teething is the answer. It is one of the most common causes of unexplained fussiness in this age window, and yet most parents are caught completely off guard when it begins — because the first signs arrive weeks before a single tooth actually appears.

This guide will walk you through exactly when Indian babies typically start teething, what the warning signs look like, what you can safely do to help, and when it is worth picking up the phone to call your pediatrician.

---

## What Age Do Babies Start Teething?

Most babies cut their first tooth somewhere between **4 and 7 months** of age. That said, the range is wider than most people expect. Some babies sprout a tooth as early as 3 months; others do not show one until after their first birthday. Both ends of that spectrum are generally considered normal.

The Indian Academy of Pediatrics (IAP) notes that teething typically begins around 6 months, with most children completing their full set of 20 primary (milk) teeth by the time they are 2.5 to 3 years old.

What tends to confuse parents is that **teething symptoms often begin 2 to 8 weeks before the tooth actually breaks through the gum**. So your baby may be drooling and chewing frantically at 3.5 months without a tooth in sight — and that is completely normal.

## The Baby Teething Timeline: All 20 Primary Teeth

The table below shows the typical eruption sequence for primary teeth. Think of these as averages, not deadlines. Every baby has their own schedule.

| Teeth | Location | When They Usually Appear |
|---|---|---|
| Lower central incisors | Bottom front (2 teeth) | 6–10 months |
| Upper central incisors | Top front (2 teeth) | 8–12 months |
| Upper lateral incisors | Top, beside front teeth (2 teeth) | 9–13 months |
| Lower lateral incisors | Bottom, beside front teeth (2 teeth) | 10–16 months |
| Upper first molars | Upper back (2 teeth) | 13–19 months |
| Lower first molars | Lower back (2 teeth) | 14–18 months |
| Upper canines | Upper, between laterals and molars (2 teeth) | 16–22 months |
| Lower canines | Lower, between laterals and molars (2 teeth) | 17–23 months |
| Lower second molars | Lower back (2 teeth) | 23–31 months |
| Upper second molars | Upper back (2 teeth) | 25–33 months |

The lower front teeth almost always arrive first. After that, teeth tend to come in pairs — one on each side — though not always at the exact same time.

## 7 Early Warning Signs of Teething in Babies

### 1. Excessive Drooling

This is usually the first thing parents notice. Saliva production increases significantly as teething begins. You will likely go through several muslin bibs a day. The drool itself can also cause a mild rash around the chin and neck — keep the area gently patted dry.

### 2. Chewing on Everything

Babies suddenly want to gnaw on fingers, toys, clothing, the edge of their crib — anything within reach. This is their instinctive response to the pressure building under the gum. Providing a safe teether gives them a purposeful outlet for this urge.

### 3. Swollen or Tender Gums

Run a clean finger gently over your baby's lower gum. If you feel a hard ridge or slight swelling, a tooth is likely on its way. The gum may also look slightly red or inflamed in that area.

### 4. Increased Fussiness and Crying

The discomfort is real. Teething pain is typically described (by pediatric dentists) as a dull, constant pressure rather than sharp pain, but for a baby who cannot yet communicate, it is distressing. Fussiness tends to peak about 3 to 5 days before the tooth erupts, then eases once it breaks through.

### 5. Disrupted Sleep

A baby who was sleeping relatively well may suddenly wake more often at night. The discomfort that is manageable during a busy, stimulating day becomes much harder to ignore in a quiet, dark room.

### 6. Pulling at Ears or Rubbing Cheeks

Pain in the jaw can radiate toward the ear and cheek. Some babies tug at their ears during teething — though it is important to rule out an ear infection as well, especially if your baby also has a fever or cold symptoms.

### 7. Mild Increase in Body Temperature

**A note from the IAP:** Teething may cause a very slight rise in temperature (below 38 degrees Celsius), but it does not cause a true fever. If your baby's temperature reads 38°C or above, that is not teething — it is a sign of illness and warrants a call to your pediatrician. This distinction matters, because dismissing a genuine fever as "just teething" can delay necessary treatment.

## What You Can Safely Do to Help

### Gum Massage

Wash your hands thoroughly, then use a clean fingertip to apply gentle, firm pressure to your baby's gum. Many babies find this soothing, at least for a few minutes. It works on the same principle as the teether — counter-pressure on the gum.

### Chilled (Not Frozen) Teethers

A slightly cooled teether can help numb the gum and reduce inflammation. Place it in the refrigerator — not the freezer. Frozen teethers are too hard and can damage delicate gum tissue and emerging teeth.

### Distraction

Sometimes the most effective tool is a change of scene. Step outside, switch activities, offer a new toy. A distracted baby is a less fussy baby — and a slightly less exhausted parent.

### What to Avoid

- **Benzocaine-based teething gels** (products that "numb" the gum): The IAP advises against these for children under 2, as benzocaine has been linked to a rare but serious condition called methemoglobinemia.
- **Amber teething necklaces**: These pose a clear choking and strangulation risk and have no proven efficacy. No reputable pediatric body recommends them.
- **Homeopathic teething tablets**: Formulations vary widely, and there is no consistent evidence of safety or benefit in infants.

## When to Call Your Pediatrician

Teething is normal, but some symptoms are not teething — and it is worth knowing the difference. Contact your pediatrician if:

- Your baby has a fever of 38°C or above
- There is no sign of any tooth by 18 months of age
- Your baby refuses feeds for more than 24 hours
- You notice swelling on the face or jaw
- Your baby seems to be in significant, unrelenting pain
- Your baby has diarrhoea lasting more than a day (loose stools can accompany teething mildly, but prolonged diarrhoea is not caused by teething)

**First dental visit:** The IAP recommends scheduling your baby's first dental appointment around their first birthday, or within 6 months of the first tooth appearing — whichever comes first. Early dental visits help establish healthy habits and catch any issues before they become problems.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What age do babies start teething?**
Most babies begin teething between 4 and 7 months, with the lower front teeth typically appearing first. However, some babies start as early as 3 months or as late as 12 months, and both are considered within the normal range.

**What are the first signs of teething in babies?**
The earliest signs are usually excessive drooling, increased chewing on hands and objects, and swollen or tender gums. Fussiness and disrupted sleep often follow as the tooth gets closer to breaking through the surface.

**Can teething start at 3 months?**
Yes, teething symptoms — particularly drooling and chewing — can begin as early as 3 months, even though the actual tooth may not appear for several more weeks. This early phase is sometimes called "pre-teething," and it is entirely normal.

**How long does teething last?**
The teething process in total lasts from roughly 4 to 6 months of age all the way through to about 30 to 33 months, when the second molars typically come in. Each individual tooth usually causes 3 to 5 days of noticeable discomfort before erupting, after which symptoms ease.

**Is it safe to use a teether for a newborn?**
Most teethers are designed for babies who are 3 months and older, when the chewing urge begins. For very young babies, always check the age recommendation on the product. Choose teethers made from food-grade silicone that are BIS-certified and free from BPA, PVC, and phthalates, and always supervise your baby during use.
